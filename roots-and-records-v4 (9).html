🔗 Relationship Map
Visual network of your ancestors — connections, witnesses, neighbors, repeated surnames
The Relationship Map shows a web-style diagram of everyone in your tree and how they connect. Add people first using + Add Person, then the nodes populate automatically. Click any node to see connections. Look for repeated surnames and shared locations — these often reveal hidden family connections African American research frequently uncovers.
🔗 How to Use the Network Map
Nodes = each person in your tree
Lines = direct family connections
Click a node = see that person's connections
Cluster patterns = indicate shared ancestry

In African American genealogy, look for people with the same surname as an enslaver — formerly enslaved people often took the surname of the person who enslaved them, which means unrelated people in your tree may share surnames from the same plantation.

📌 Always Active — African American Research Priorities
🏛️
Freedmen's Bureau Records — All Four Lines
1.6 million FREE records: labor contracts, marriage registers, ration rolls 1865–1872. Search for Foreman, Drewitt, Smith, Reid. Search FamilySearch FREE →
📜
1870 Census — First Post-Emancipation Census
This is where freed ancestors appear by name for the first time. Search all four lines: Foreman/Drewitt (Lawson), Smith (Elsie), Reid (Charlie), Smith (Erma). Search 1870 Census FREE →
📜
1880 Census — Verify Every Family Member
The 1880 census shows full household including ages and relationships. Most detailed pre-1900 census. Free on FamilySearch. Search 1880 Census FREE →
⚔️
USCT Military Records — Paternal Lines
United States Colored Troops 1863–1866. Foreman and Reid lines — check if male ancestors served. Pension files often contain the most detailed personal records available. Search NARA USCT →
📰
Newspaper Search — Your Surnames
Search Chronicling America (FREE) for Foreman, Drewitt, Lawson, Smith, and Reid in Southern states 1865–1920. Obituaries, land notices, and church news appear here. Search Chronicling America →
🧬
DNA — Upload to GEDmatch for Cross-Platform Matching
GEDmatch lets you match against people from Ancestry, 23andMe, FTDNA, and MyHeritage all at once. Free tier includes One-to-Many comparison. Go to GEDmatch → · Upload Raw DNA →
🔤
Soundex — Check Spelling Variants
Foreman, Drewitt, Lawson, Smith, Reid may appear as Forman, Drewit, Lawsen, Smyth, Reed in census records. Always search Soundex variants. Go to Soundex Tool →
📊
1870 Wall Strategy — Before Emancipation
Before 1870, enslaved ancestors appear only as numbers in the slave schedule. To cross this wall: (1) Identify the enslaver's family in same county 1860 slave schedule, (2) Search Freedmen's Bureau for same county, (3) Look for plantation name in USCT pension files. Ask AI Brick Wall Breaker →

🎨 Leeds Clustering
Group DNA matches into 4 color clusters — one per grandparent line
How the Leeds Method Works
1. Take all your DNA matches between 90–400 cM (typically 2nd–3rd cousins)
2. Sort them by cM (largest first)
3. Start a new color group with the top match
4. If a subsequent match shares DNA with someone in an existing group → put them in that group
5. If they don't share with any group → start a new color group
6. End result: 4 clusters = your 4 grandparent lines
